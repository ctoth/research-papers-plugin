## Summary

This paper publishes STROBE, a reporting guideline for the three major analytic observational-study designs: cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional studies. Its core deliverable is a 22-item checklist covering title/abstract, introduction, methods, results, discussion, and funding disclosure. Eighteen items are shared across all three designs and four contain design-specific guidance. A separate Explanation and Elaboration document and design-specific checklist variants are provided for operational use. *(pp.1-3)*

The paper is explicit that STROBE is a reporting standard, not a prescription for how to design or analyze observational studies. The aim is to make published reports complete enough for readers to understand what was planned, what was done, what was found, and how generalisable and bias-prone the findings may be. *(pp.1, 3)*

## Problem And Motivation

The authors start from a practical failure mode: much biomedical research is observational, but published reports often omit essential details, which makes it hard to judge strengths, weaknesses, confounding, bias, external validity, and the meaning of the results. They cite both the scale of observational research and the dependence of systematic reviews and evidence-based decisions on transparent reporting as the rationale for the initiative. *(pp.1-2)*

They also argue that observational studies are especially vulnerable to underreporting because potential confounders and analytic choices are often not reported clearly, and because many journals do not explain study rationale or methods in enough detail. This makes observational evidence difficult to appraise, compare, or synthesize. *(p.2)*

## How STROBE Was Developed

The STROBE Initiative was established in 2004. The group reviewed existing guidance, empirical studies of reporting quality, and methodological work, then used a two-day workshop in Bristol in September 2004 plus repeated drafting, meetings, conference calls, and e-mail review to converge on the checklist. Workshop participants included methodologists, researchers, and journal editors; a larger contributor group reviewed drafts after the meeting. *(p.2)*

The authors explicitly borrowed from prior reporting-guideline efforts such as CONSORT and related extensions, but adapted the checklist to the problems specific to observational research. They stress that empirical evidence was used where available and informed consensus where evidence was incomplete. *(pp.1-2)*

## STROBE Checklist

Table 1 defines the checklist items. The checklist is organized by manuscript section and is meant to be used alongside the separate Explanation and Elaboration paper. *(pp.2-3)*

### Title And Abstract

| Item | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 1a | Indicate the study's design with a commonly used term in the title or abstract. |
| 1b | Provide in the abstract an informative and balanced summary of what was done and what was found. |

### Introduction

| Item | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | Explain the scientific background and rationale for the investigation. |
| 3 | State specific objectives, including prespecified hypotheses. |

### Methods

| Item | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 4 | Present key elements of study design early in the paper. |
| 5 | Describe setting, locations, and relevant dates, including recruitment, exposure, follow-up, and data-collection periods. |
| 6 | Describe participant eligibility criteria and the sources/methods of participant selection; also report cohort follow-up methods, case-control case ascertainment and control selection with rationale, or cross-sectional participant-selection methods as appropriate. |
| 7 | Clearly define outcomes, exposures, predictors, potential confounders, effect modifiers, and, when relevant, diagnostic criteria. |
| 8 | For each variable of interest, give data sources and measurement methods; if multiple groups exist, describe comparability of assessment methods. |
| 9 | Describe efforts to address potential bias. |
| 10 | Explain how the study size was determined. |
| 11 | Explain how quantitative variables were handled in the analyses, including any groupings and why they were chosen. |
| 12 | Describe statistical methods, including confounding control, subgroup and interaction analyses, missing-data handling, design-specific handling of loss to follow-up or matching or sampling strategy, and sensitivity analyses. |

### Results

| Item | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 13 | Report participant flow at each stage: numbers potentially eligible, examined, eligible, included, followed up, and analysed, plus reasons for non-participation and use of a flow diagram. |
| 14 | Give descriptive data on participant characteristics, exposures, potential confounders, and the number of participants with missing data; for cohort studies, summarize follow-up time. |
| 15 | Report outcome data: outcome events or summary measures over time for cohort studies, exposure/category counts or summary measures for case-control studies, and outcome-event or summary-measure counts for cross-sectional studies. |
| 16 | Give main results, including unadjusted and, when applicable, confounder-adjusted estimates with precision and a clear statement of which confounders were adjusted for and why; report category boundaries when continuous variables were categorized; if relevant, translate relative-risk estimates into absolute risk for a meaningful time period. |
| 17 | Report other analyses, such as subgroup analyses, interaction analyses, and sensitivity analyses. |

### Discussion And Other Information

| Item | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| 18 | Summarize key results with reference to study objectives. |
| 19 | Discuss limitations of the study, taking into account sources of potential bias or imprecision and both the direction and magnitude of possible bias. |
| 20 | Give a cautious overall interpretation of results considering objectives, limitations, multiplicity of analyses, results from similar studies, and other relevant evidence. |
| 21 | Discuss the generalisability of the study results. |
| 22 | Give the source of funding and the role of funders for the present study and, if applicable, for the original study on which the article is based. |

Some checklist items are marked as design-specific and are written separately for cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional studies. The paper notes that separate checklists for the three study designs were made available on the STROBE website. *(pp.2-3)*

## Important Limits And Interpretation Rules

The authors repeatedly say STROBE should not be misread as a tool for scoring the methodological quality of observational studies. In the companion Explanation and Elaboration paper they intentionally avoid using the phrase "quality of reporting" as if good reporting guaranteed good methods; the main goal is transparent reporting so readers can judge strengths, weaknesses, confounding, bias, and generalisability more clearly. *(p.3)*

They also resist turning the checklist into a rigid manuscript template. Checklist items should be addressed with enough clarity and detail to allow critical review and interpretation, but authors do not need to present them in a fixed order or under fixed headings. The authors even encourage narrative elements so papers remain informative and readable rather than becoming standardized, style-stripped forms. *(p.3)*

Another boundary condition is scope. STROBE does not try to prescribe how to design observational studies or which methods are best. The group excluded some topics because evidence was insufficient, because some influences on reporting were not representative across geography or discipline, or because the issues were outside the reporting remit. The checklist is treated as revisable as new evidence and criticism accumulate. *(p.3)*

## Implications For This Project

For this repository, the paper is useful as a reporting ontology for observational-study papers rather than as an algorithm or model paper. The 22-item checklist is directly usable as a schema for extraction targets, completeness checks, or note templates for cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional studies. *(pp.2-3)*

Several checklist items are especially implementation-relevant for structured extraction:

1. Participant-selection logic is design dependent, so extraction templates should branch for cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional studies instead of forcing one universal field layout. *(p.2)*
2. Measurement and analysis fields should explicitly capture confounders, effect modifiers, missing-data handling, subgroup analyses, and sensitivity analyses rather than treating "methods" as one undifferentiated block. *(p.2)*
3. Result extraction should preserve both unadjusted and adjusted estimates and record which confounders were included in adjusted analyses. *(p.2)*
4. Interpretation fields should separate key results, limitations, cautious interpretation, generalisability, and funding rather than collapsing them into a generic discussion summary. *(pp.2-3)*

## Usefulness For This Collection

Moderate to high usefulness. The paper is not an NLP or machine-learning contribution, but it is a strong source if the project needs a defensible checklist for observational-study reporting, structured extraction targets for epidemiologic papers, or a rubric for judging whether observational reports contain enough information for downstream synthesis and critique. *(pp.1-3)*
